How We Got To The Modern Bike
Mar 28, 2016 Tweet

The evolution of the bicycle is a great example of the design process: Make something that works with the materials you have, adjust and experiment, and keep the best parts.

Here are illustrations of that evolution starting with the first known bike in 1810.

1810 German hobby horse

With no pedals or steering, the rider simply scooted along with his feet on the ground.

1815 Dandy horse

Less bulky than the hobby horse and and with limited steering, the rider sat on the saddle and walked/ran.

1818 Pedestrian Curricle

This update has more sophisticated steering and a comfortable seat.

1839 Self-propelling velocipede

With this design the rider could finally take his feet off the ground, propelling the vehicle by pumping the pedals.

1869 French bone-shaker

Named after how badly riders were jostled about, this stylish bicycle introduced rotating pedals.

1872 Penny farthing

This funny-looking bicycle got more distance per rotation but it required good balance.

1885 American Star

This update to the penny farthing raised the pedals for easier reach and attached the pedals to the back wheel and the steering to the front wheel.

1889 Safety bicycle

The introduction of gears brought down the size of the wheels. Small suspension springs and hand-held brakes made for less bumpy and safer ride.

1899 Updated safety bicycle

The rider's weight was moved back and the wheels are made equal size.

1920 Road bike

The lowered handles brings the rider into a more aerodynamic position.

1970 Raleigh Grand Prix

An adjustable seat and hand-pumped brakes made this easy for anyone to ride.

2015 Fuji Flat Back road bike

A light frame, thin tires and multiple gears make for a fast ride.
