5 Keys to Getting Smarter
July 16, 2016 Tweet

Scientific American recently published a study that explored if you can increase your intelligence. The good news: you can!

According to the study, anyone can increasing fluidity in their brain, gaining greater capacity for learning and complex thought.

There are 5 activities you should do regularly.

1.

"The joy of life comes from our encounters with new experiences, and hence there is no greater joy than to have an endlessly changing horizon, for each day to have a new and different sun.”

― Jon Krakauer, Into the Wild

You get smarter by upsetting your habits. If you read a lot, divert some of that time to doing something physical. If you've been focussing on large things (cars, trees), turn to something delicate (needlepoint). If you've been indoors, go outside. Do things you don't know if you'll be good at and you aren't even sure you'll like. By putting your mind in new environments your brain will gain dexterity and resilience.

2.

"One should not pursue goals that are easily achieved. One must develop an instinct for what one can just barely achieve through one's greatest efforts."

—Albert Einstein

Engaging in a hard task - reading a dense book, solving a Sudoku puzzle - is beneficial to the brain only for as long as it's uncomfortable. The benefits wear out the minute you find the task easy. During your first game of Tetris your brain was developing and strengthening as you figured out how to fit the shapes together. But once you got good at it all development stopped. After that you relied on memorized patterns. So keep moving on to harder tasks. Embrace the feeling of discomfort when doing something difficult. That's your brain strengthening.

3.

“I never made one of my discoveries through the process of rational thinking.”

― Albert Einstein

When thinking about a problem, don't just rely on memorized or only relevant facts. Pull in everything you know. Make connections between the logical, social, aesthetic and practical parts of you. Engaging all part of your brain creates neural pathways, and the more pathways, the greater the fluidity.

4.

“Don't wish it were easier. Wish you were better.”

― Jim Rohn

Efficiency is the enemy to brain training. Avoid it. Compose an email without using the spell-checker. Drive somewhere you've never been with the GPS turned off. Translate a foreign-language website manually, without Google Translate. Work. Be self-reliant.

5.

“First of all," he said, "if you can learn a simple trick, Scout, you'll get along a lot better with all kinds of folks. You never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view--until you climb into his skin and walk around in it.”

― Harper Lee, To Kill a Mockingbird

Expose yourself to new people so that you are thrown into situations in which to practice the skills mentioned above. You might learn new ideas and you will definitely hear yourself articulate your own. With the emotional benefits that also come with being social, there's a lot to gain from getting out there!

So go ahead and get strong in the brain. You can do it.
