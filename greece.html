If the Ancient Greeks Are to be Believed...
Mon Jan 18, 2016
Tweet

If you ever read The Iliad in school you might have overlooked some odd passages regarding colors: where it says the sky is bronze, the sea is the color of wine, and sheep are violet. Something got lost in translation, right?

Not necessarily. Most ancient texts, including the New Testament, use colors in ways that don't match how you and I see the world. The reason might be that a different culture sees differently, and color perception depends on if there is a word for a particular color in its language. If your brain doesn't have a word for blue - like the ancients didn't - it won't detect blue and instead substitute a color for which it does have a word.

Sounds outlandish but convincing evidence was videoed in the Himba Color Experment. A tribe in Namibia has no word for blue but several for green and the Himba volunteers couldn't pick out a blue swatch among a bunch of green swatches.

It's not conclusive but very tempting. I mean, who wouldn't want to live in a world with violet sheep?
