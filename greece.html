If the Ancient Greeks Are to be Believed...
Mon Jan 18, 2016
Tweet

If you ever read The Iliad in school you might have overlooked some odd passages regarding colors: where it says the sheep are violet, the sky is bronze, and the sea is the color of wine. Most ancient texts, from the Odyssey to the New Testament, ascribe funny colors to the world they are describing.

Something got lost in translation, right?

Not necessarily. There is evidence that past cultures (and some present ones) actually saw colors differently from how you and I see them. Color perception might depend on if there is a word for a particular color in a given language. If a people don't have a word for blue - like the ancient Greeks didn't - they won't detect blue. Instead they will see a close-enough color for which they do have a word.

It sounds outlandish but convincing evidence was videoed in the Himba Color Experment. A tribe in Namibia has no word for blue but has several words for green. The Himba volunteers were unable to pick out a blue swatch among a bunch of green swatches. They all looked green to them.

It's not conclusive but very tempting. I mean, who wouldn't want to live in a world with violet sheep?
