For your webpage, logo or bedroom, you might be scared your color choices don't pass graphics muster. I say if it feels good do it, but for you perfectionists here are steps to a fail-proof color palette.

We'll start big: with a 4-color palette. In a later article we'll cover two and three-color palette.

1. Decide on a Color Wheel

The color wheel you choose has the biggest influence on your color palette. There are a lot of color wheels to choose from. The differences boil down to a few factors:

a. Web-optimized vs. Print-optimized

Web-optimized wheels use the primary colors red, green and blue. They are best for digital devices (computers, watches, and anything else with an LED screen).

Print-optimized wheels use the primary colors red, yellow, and blue and are best for paper, textiles and other tangible materials.

In this tutorial we'll use a web-optimized wheel. We'll cover a print-optimized wheel in another tutorial.

b. Pure vs. Toned/Tinted

In a pure wheel the primary colors don't contain traces of other colors. The red is nothing but red. The blue is nothing but blue. The result is a very bright wheel.

Other wheels may come with a gray tone or pastel whiteness. These treatments takes away the bright and adds some seriousness or lightness.

I think it's best to use a pure color wheel. You can always add tones and tints later.

c. Number of Colors

A color wheel can have as few as three colors or as many as 3,000. Too few colors is not very exciting but too many are hard to tell apart.

I like a color wheel with 24 colors.

2. Pick a Base Color

A quick way to make decisions about your color palette is to pick a base color and let the rest follow from that.

Choose a base color now. I'm choosing golden-yellow, third from the top red.

3. Choose a Color Scheme

Now decide on a color scheme. For four colors, you have two schemes to choose from.

a. Tetradic

Tetradic schemes are vibrant, balanced and contrasting. They consist of a base color and and three colors that are 60 degrees, 180 degrees, and 240 degrees from the base color. Google, Ebay, and Microsoft use this color scheme.
