How the Red Coat Got Its Red
Mar 13, 2016
Tweet

Red has been the color of regality and nobility for centuries. Henry VIII was known for his red robes, and Cardinals showed off their rank with red cloaks and hats. Unbeknownst to many who wore it, the red was the residue of crushed Mexican insects.


In the 15th century, Spain colonized much of Mexico and South America. Their goals were pride and profit. They got both by capitalizing on the natives' bright red clothes.

The secret to the lush redness was a parasite that lived in cactus pads: the cochineal (pronounced ko-shuh-'neel).

Farmers collected cactus pads, scraped off the bugs, crushed them into powder, and added water to make dye.

Europe hadn't had a rich source of red before and Spain now had a monopoly on the color. They made a fortune selling red dye to its neighbors, particularly the English who loved using colors to show off social rank.

Small problem for England: Spain was a political rival and it pained the British crown to give money to Spain. The solution: the crown made it illegal for anyone except the nobility to wear red. This limited trade but kept the upper classes well-dressed.

For centries, Spain kept secret where they got this powder and what it was made of.
