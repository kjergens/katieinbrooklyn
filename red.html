How the Red Coat Got Its Red
Mar 13, 2016
Tweet

For centuries red has symbolized royalty and nobility. Henry VIII favored his red robes; Cardinals showed off their rank with red cloaks and hats; and we all remember the British red coats.

Unbeknownst to those who wore it, the red in these expensive garments was the residue of crushed Mexican bugs.


In the 15th century, Spain colonized much of Mexico and South America. Their goals were pride and profit. They got both by capitalizing on the natives' bright red clothes.

The secret to the lush redness was a parasite that lived in cactus pads: the cochineal (pronounced ko-shuh-'neel). Farmers collected cactus pads, scraped off the bugs, crushed them into powder, and added water to make dye.

Europe hadn't had a rich source of red and Spain now had a monopoly on the color. They made a fortune selling red dye throughout Europe, particularly to the English who loved using colors to show off social rank.

But there was a small problem for England: Spain was a political rival. England didn't want to be loyal customers to their enemy. So, to curb trade with Spain, the crown made it illegal for anyone except nobility and military to wear red.

For centries, Spain kept secret where they got the red powder and what it was made of.
