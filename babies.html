What Do Babies See?
Jul 10, 2016 Tweet

When babies are born they are extremely near-sighted. They can only see two feet in front of them, and even in that range everything is reduced to primary colors and shades of gray.

The problem isn't with their eyes, which are fully-developed at birth. The optical nerve sends accurate messages to the brain from day one. The problem is that the brain isn't fully developed. It doesn't know how to interpret the visual messages it's getting. It will take months for the brain to learn how to make sense of the information the eyes are giving it.

Here is the journey your baby's vision takes in the first eight months.

1st month

In the first month vision is very blurry. Focus limited to two feet in front of the face, beyond which the baby can only detect silhouettes and motion. The baby can only see primary colors. The eyes may not work together, which means there is no depth perception.


2nd month

In the second month the baby can detect secondary colors: orange, green, purple. Focus improves. The eyes sporadically work together the baby can on-and-off detect foreground versus background.


3rd month

By the third month the baby can see several feet away. Eyes can work together and can track a moving object. The baby sees the difference between similar colors and can make out detailed designs.


5th month

In the fifth month the eyes work together even better and can start to see levels of depth. This is when babies practice depth-perception by grabbing cups and pulling hair. They see small items, like crumbs on a floor and cracks on a sidwalk. They see faces from across the room, even if they can't identify them. They can recognize an item even if it's partially out of view or in different lighting.


8th month

At eight months old, vision has almost completely matured. The baby can detect a wide range of colors including pastels. She can accurately judge distances and recognize faces from several feet away.
