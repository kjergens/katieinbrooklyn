What Do Babies See?
Jul 10, 2016
Tweet

A baby's first look at the world is a blur. She can only see basic colors and can't focus on objects more than two feet away.

The problem isn't with her eyes, which are fully-developed at birth. The problem is with her brain. At birth the brain is not ready to interpret messages sent by the optical nerve.

A baby's brain spends the first three months of life tirelessly making sense of visual information and storing those patterns for later. It takes another five months to fine-tune the brain-eye communication and by eight months a baby cas see almost as well as an adult.

1st month

Vision is blurry. Focus limited to two feet in front of the face, beyond which the baby can only detect silhouettes and motion. The baby can only see primary colors. The eyes may not work together.

2nd month

Baby can start to see secondary colors: orange, green, purple. Focus improves.

3rd month

Baby can see farther away. Eyes start working together and can track a moving object. Can see more colors and more detailed designs.

5th month

Eyes start to work together and start to see depth. This is when babies practice depth-perception by grabbing cups and pulling hair. They see small items, like crumbs on a floor and cracks on a sidwalk. They see faces from across the room, even if they can't identify them. They can recognize an item even if it's partially out of view or in different lighting.

8th month

Vision has almost completely matured. They can distinguish pastels, accurately judge distances and recognize faces from several feet away.
