For your webpage, logo or bedroom, you might be scared your color choices don't pass graphics muster. I say if it feels good do it, but for you perfectionists here are steps to a fail-proof color palette.

1. Find a Color Wheel & Stick With It

The best way to guarantee a successful palette is to find or make a color wheel then pick all your colors from it. There are a lot of color wheels to choose from. The differences boil down to a few factors:

Web vs. Print

Digital, or web-optimized, color wheels use the primary colors red, blue and green. They are best for digital devices (computers, phones, game consoles) because they use the primary colors of light.

Print-optimized color wheels use the primary colors red, blue and yellow. They are best for print and textiles but are still very popular in the digital world.



Both can work nicely in either medium so it's a matter of taste which one you use.

In Part 1 and Part 2 of this series we'll use a web-optimized wheel. In Part 3 and Part 4 we'll explore a print-optimized wheel.

Pure vs. Tinted

A pure color wheel has primary colors that don't contain traces of any other color. The red is nothing but red. The blue is nothing but blue. The result is a very bright wheel.

Non-pure or tinted color wheels have primary colors in which other hues have been added, affecting the colors in between. The wheel may have a gray tone, pastel whiteness or some other tint.



I think it's best to use a pure color wheel. You can always add tones and tints later.

Number of Color Slices

A color wheel can have as few as three colors or as many as 3,000. Too few colors is not very exciting but too many are hard to tell apart.

I like a color wheel with 24 colors.

For this tutorial I made a web-optimized pure color wheel with 24 colors which we'll use in the steps below.

A quick way to make a color palette is to pick a starting color as a springboard. Choose any starting color now.

3. Apply a Color Scheme

Finally, apply a color scheme to get the rest of the palette. For just two-color palettes you can use the complementary scheme. For three-color palettes choose between triadic, split-complementary, and analogous.

Complementary

The complementary color scheme uses two colors, a base color and the color 180 degrees from it.
