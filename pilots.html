Designing an Image: The Pilot
Wed Mar 9, 2016
Tweet

Pilots used to look like this:

They were weathered, rugged and made their own rules: the epitome of 1910s masculinity. After all they had one of the world's most dangerous jobs. These were not fighter-pilots, but the corps of U.S. Air Mail carriers.

Fortune magazine summed up Air Mail pilots:

"When they play poker they play all night. When they smoke they smoke too much. When they drink their glasses leak, and when they make love complaints are rare."

In the 1920s all that changed.

Plane manufacturers wanted to sell to individuals. They needed the public to have a safer image of planes and flying. To that end they took out magazine ads and sponsored cross-oceanic flights featuring pilots who were a bit different from their maverick predecessors.

Because of these campaigns women pilots - "aviatrixes", lady birds", "lipstick fliers", call them what you will - were all the rage in the 1920s.

The Ladies Aeronautical Society proclaimed:

"There is no finer past-time for a respectable young woman than to get out in the fresh air and exercise mind and body in the pursuit of the mechanodynamic sciences of flight!"

The image became so powerful and commonplace it was regularly used in ads for clothing, cigarettes, gum and more.

In the 1950s things changed again.

This time commercial airlines got involved. The popular idea that flying is a hobby for young women contradicted their message: planes are serious business.

So once again the pilot was re-imagined.

Airlines groomed their pilots to be male and thin. They wore business suits to look professional and a naval caps to look responsible. Lastly, they spoke over an intercom: a faceless voice of authority. The pilot was now a father figure.

This latest pilot image has endured for over 60 years.
