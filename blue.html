A Giant Step Closer to Elusive Pure Blue
Jul 4, 2016
Tweet

Humanity has long been in pursuit of blue. Nature doesn't provide many blue materials - some peacock feathers, a few rocks - and throughout history producing the pigment was achieved only by the very crafty.

Early Egyptians made azurite, which became so popular it reached paintings in Europe and Asia. Afghanistan later took over the blue market with ultramarine. Then modern chemistry produced cobalt and cerulean.

All of these compounds reflect blue to some degree. Some reflect light in a way that looks violet-blue (ultramarine). Others reflect a greenish-blue (cerulean). Out of reach was the compound to reflect blue and nothing else.

Until now.

Chemists out of Oregon State University have stumbled upon a compound that reflects almost exclusively blue. They call it YInMn (pronounced yin-min) blue.

It was discovered in 2009 but is just coming to market as a pigment now.

You might be thinking, "Couldn't I always make pure blue on my computer screen?" Yes, you could, but there hasn't been a physical material available to print it.

Hurruh! Now we can make tangible very-blue things. If you are not a color-enthusiast should you care? Probably. Other than its blueness, YInMn has properties with practical importance: It doesn't fade over time, it reflects infrared rays, and, unlike similar pigments like colbalt, is non-toxic and non-carcinogenic. This means it has potential to cool homes (be on the look-out for blue roofs) and be a safe ingredient for paint and clothes.
