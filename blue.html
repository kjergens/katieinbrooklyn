A Giant Step Closer to Elusive Pure Blue
Dec 4, 2016 Tweet

Humanity has long been in pursuit of blue. Nature doesn't provide many blue materials - just some peacock feathers and a few stones - which makes creating a pigment a tricky feat.

Early Egyptians made azurite by weathering copper ore deposits. Afghanistan later gave us ultramarine, made from a crushed precious stone. Modern chemists sythetically created cerulean and cobalt.


The problem is none of these materials was actually blue. A truly blue object absorbs all the other colors in the light spectrum, leaving only blue to be reflected back. These pigments of the past didn't fully absorb the other colors, tainting the blue that got reflected back. Some reflected violet-blue (azurite and ultramarine); others greenish-blue (cerulean). No material had reflected pure blue.

Until now.


Chemists at Oregon State University stumbled upon a compound that reflects almost exclusively blue. They named it YInMn (pronounced yin-min) blue. It was discovered in 2009 but is just coming to market as a pigment now.

You might be thinking, "Couldn't I always make pure blue on my computer screen?" Yes, you could have, but there hadn't been a physical material available to print it.



Hurruh! Now we can make very-blue things. That's not where the story ends, either. YInMn also reflects infrared rays, doesn't fade over time and, unlike similar pigments like cobalt, is non-toxic and non-carcinogenic. This means it has potential to cool homes and be a safer ingredient for paint and clothes.
