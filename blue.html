A Giant Step Closer to Elusive Pure Blue
Jul 4, 2016
Tweet

Humanity has long been in pursuit of blue. Nature doesn't provide many blue materials to work with: some peacock feathers, a few rocks, but not much else. Throughout history producing the pigment was achieved only by the very crafty or very wealthy.

Early Egyptians made azurite from copper. Afghanistan later took over the blue market with ultramarine, made from a crushed precious stone. Modern chemistry replaced natural pigments with cobalt and cerulean.

A compound is considered blue when it fully absorbs all the other colors in the light spectrum, leaving only blue to be reflected back. The materials we had so far didn't fully absorb the other colors, tainting the blue that got reflected back. Some reflect violet-blue (azurite and ultramarine). Others reflect greenish-blue (cerulean). No material had reflected pure blue.

Until now.

Chemists out of Oregon State University stumbled upon a compound that reflects almost exclusively blue. They call it YInMn (pronounced yin-min) blue. It was discovered in 2009 but is just coming to market as a pigment now.

You might be thinking, "Couldn't I always make pure blue on my computer screen?" Yes, you could, but there hasn't been a physical material available to print it.

Hurruh! Now we can make very-blue things. If you are not a color-enthusiast should you care? Probably. Other than its blueness, YInMn has practical properties: It doesn't fade over time, it reflects infrared rays, and, unlike similar pigments like colbalt, is non-toxic and non-carcinogenic. This means it has potential to cool homes (be on the look-out for blue roofs) and be a safe ingredient for paint and clothes.
